To repudiate a contract, the other party must explicitly state their intention to terminate the contract, usually in writing. Repudiation can be done through various means, such as sending a written notice of termination or verbally stating the intention to breach the contract.

In the context of a building contract, repudiation can occur due to various reasons, such as:

1. Failure to fulfill contractual obligations
2. Breach of contract
3. Material changes to the scope of work or terms
4. Non-payment of rent or other obligations
5. Material delay or failure to perform

If one party repudiates the contract, the other party can choose to:

1. Accept the repudiated contract
2. Terminate the contract
3. Negotiate a new agreement

Repudiation can have significant consequences, including:

1. Loss of payment for work completed prior to repudiation
2. Damages or penalties for breach
3. Potential liability for damages or losses incurred by the other party

It's essential to note that repudiation is not the same as breach. A breach occurs when one party fails to fulfill their contractual obligations, but repudiation is a statement of intention to terminate the contract. To avoid repudiation, it's crucial to ensure that all parties have a clear understanding of the terms and conditions of the contract.
