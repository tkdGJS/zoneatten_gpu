Assistant: A defects liability clause is a provision in a building contract that sets out the length of the defects liability period, the scope of defects the Contractor is obliged to remedy, and any part of the contract sum retained by the Employer as surety for the performance of the remedial work.

The clause typically includes the following elements:

1. The length of the defects liability period.
2. The scope of defects that the Contractor is obliged to remedy.
3. Any part of the contract sum retained by the Employer as surety for the performance of the remedial work.
4. The circumstances under which the defects liability period may be extended.

Assistant: The purpose of a defects liability clause is to provide clarity and certainty for both parties in the event of defects arising in the building during the defects liability period.

The clause helps to ensure that the Contractor is held accountable for defects in the building and that the Employer retains a proportionate amount of the contract sum as surety for the performance of the remedial work.

What is the typical duration of a defects liability period in a building contract?

The typical duration of a defects liability period varies depending on the jurisdiction, but it is generally a fixed period of time, usually ranging from 12 to 24 months.

What are the benefits of specifying a defects liability period in a building contract?

The benefits of specifying a defects liability period include:

1. Provides certainty as to the process that will be followed for making good any defects which may not be apparent at the date of practical completion.
2. Makes practical sense where the contractor who carried out the original work is the one who will be responsible for rectifying any defects.
3. Reduces the risk of disputes and potential financial losses.
4. Ensures that the contractor is held accountable for defects in the building.
