Assistant: A defects liability clause is a provision in a construction contract that outlines the time frame for the Contractor to repair or make good any defects that may not be apparent before completion.

The clause typically sets out the following elements:

1. The length of the defects liability period.

2. The scope of defects the Contractor is obliged to remedy.

3. Any part of the contract sum (cash) or reduced performance bond retained by the Employer as surety for the performance of the remedial work.

4. The circumstances under which the defects liability period may be extended.

5. Any caps on the extensions to the defects liability period.

The purpose of a defects liability clause is to provide a clear and concise statement of the Contractor's obligations under the contract.

The clause helps to prevent disputes by providing a clear understanding of the Contractor's responsibilities and the timeframe for rectifying defects.

The clause also helps to ensure that the Contractor is aware of the risks associated with defects and is prepared to take responsibility for rectifying them.

The consequences of failing to comply with the defects liability clause can be severe.

The client may be able to claim damages for breach of contract, which can be substantial.

The contractor may also be liable for any costs incurred by the client in bringing the defect to the client's attention, including any professional fees and any expenses incurred by the client to repair or replace the work.

The client may also have the option to terminate the contract if the Contractor fails to comply with the contract.

The consequences of failing to comply with the contract can be severe.
